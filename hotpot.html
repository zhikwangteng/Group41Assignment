HotPot

calories (150)

Hot pot is a flavorful broth traditionally served inside a large metal pot. The broth is brought to a boil and left simmering for the duration of the meal. Raw ingredients, such as meat and vegetables, are placed into the simmering broth and thus cooked. The cooked pieces are dipped into dipping sauces for additional flavor. Hot pot is considered a main course and is usually served without rice or noodles on the side. Hot pots can be prepared and eaten at home or in a restaurant. Typical hot pot ingredients include thinly sliced meat, leaf vegetables, mushrooms, vermicelli, sliced potatoes, bean products, egg dumplings, tofu, and seafood. Raw ingredients are pre-sliced into thin sections that will cook quickly and consistently in the simmering broth, which is kept at a gentle boiling temperature. Most raw foods can be cooked in a hot pot, although they may have different cooking times, and must be immersed in the soup and then removed accordingly. At the conclusion of the meal, the broth would have acquired many flavors from the added ingredients and may be served to the diners after turning off the heat source.

The History of HotPot

The tripods of Zhou dynasty may be the earliest prototypes of the hot pot. Diners among the nobility each had a personal pot made of bronze, called ran lu 燃爐. The main part of ran lu was a small stove with a small pot above burning charcoal. Later, a hot pot made with copper was created during the "Three Kingdoms period" (200–280 AD), which is generally acknowledged as the origin of the hot pot. During the Qing dynasty, hot pot became popular among the emperors. In particular, the Qianlong Emperor was very fond of hot pot and would eat it for almost every meal. Later, the Jiaqing Emperor also had a banquet with 1,550 hot pots at his coronation.[4]Empress Dowager Cixi was also known to have enjoyed hot pot, especially during the winter season. Since the 1990s, as the number of Chinese immigrants entering the United States has grown significantly, Chinese food has also spread to the United States, and hot pot has become globally recognized

HotPot in Malaysia (HaiDiLao)

Hai Di Lao is a renowned hotpot chain that has garnered international acclaim for its exceptional dining experience, and its arrival in Malaysia has been nothing short of a culinary revelation. Originating from Sichuan, China, Hai Di Lao has perfected the art of hotpot, offering a unique blend of rich, flavorful broths, fresh ingredients, and a customizable dining experience that caters to all palates. In Malaysia, Hai Di Lao has quickly become a favorite destination for food lovers seeking an interactive and immersive meal. The brand is synonymous with top-tier customer service, where every detail is meticulously attended to—from the moment you step in until the time you leave. The signature "dancing noodle" performance, a delightful display of skill and entertainment, adds to the vibrant atmosphere, making every visit memorable. Patrons can indulge in a vast array of high-quality meats, seafood, vegetables, and unique sauces, all selected and prepared to perfection. Hai Di Lao's focus on hygiene and quality is evident in its open kitchen concept, where diners can observe the preparation of their meals, ensuring transparency and trust. The restaurant also offers thoughtful amenities, such as free snacks, drinks, and even manicures while waiting for a table, elevating the overall dining experience to an unparalleled level. As Hai Di Lao continues to expand its presence in Malaysia, it has firmly established itself as a culinary powerhouse, offering not just a meal, but an experience that tantalizes the senses and leaves a lasting impression. Whether you're a hotpot aficionado or a first-timer, Hai Di Lao promises an unforgettable gastronomic journey that reflects the richness of Chinese culinary heritage, all while embracing the warmth and hospitality that Malaysians hold dear.
